# COOPERATIVE STABILITY CONDITIONS
(Post-Boundary Extension within the Continuation Framework)

Status: Draft (v0.1)

---

## Introduction

The Theory of Cooperative Boundary describes the conditions under which systems transition from coexistence into cooperative coordination. When interaction risk falls below a threshold, agents begin to rely on repeated engagement, allowing coordinated behaviour to emerge.

However, crossing the cooperative boundary does not guarantee that cooperation will persist.

Once cooperation begins, systems must sustain coordination across time despite uncertainty, variability in behaviour, and changing environmental conditions. The continuation of cooperation therefore depends not only on the initial formation of coordinated interaction, but also on the stability of the structures that support it.

This section examines the conditions under which cooperative systems remain viable after the cooperative boundary has been crossed.

---

## Cooperative Stability

A cooperative system remains stable when coordinated interaction can continue across time without degrading into:

- coordination breakdown  
- signal unreliability  
- participant withdrawal  
- or extractive behaviour  

Stability does not imply the absence of change. Cooperative systems must adapt to changing conditions in order to maintain continuation. Stability therefore refers to the capacity of the system to sustain coordination while accommodating variation.

---

## Structural Conditions for Stability

The persistence of cooperative systems depends on several structural conditions. These conditions do not prescribe behaviour, but describe the constraints within which cooperation can continue.

---

### 1. Stability and Adaptive Variation

Cooperative systems operate through a combination of stabilising and adaptive processes.

Stabilising processes provide reliability and predictability. Repeated interactions, established practices, and consistent signal interpretation allow participants to form expectations about future behaviour.

Adaptive processes introduce variation. New behaviours, strategies, and coordination patterns allow systems to respond to changing conditions and replenish their capacity for continuation.

For cooperation to persist, these two processes must remain functionally distinct.

If adaptive variation directly disrupts stabilising processes, coordination becomes unstable. If stabilising processes prevent variation, the system becomes unable to respond to environmental change.

Cooperative stability therefore depends on the coexistence of stable coordination and bounded adaptive variation.

---

### 2. Signal Integrity

Cooperative systems rely on signals derived from contribution events to form expectations about future behaviour.

For coordination to persist, these signals must remain:

- interpretable  
- sufficiently reliable  
- and broadly comparable across participants  

When signals become distorted, inconsistent, or inflated, observers are no longer able to form stable expectations. As a result, trust judgements weaken and coordination becomes unstable.

Signal integrity therefore represents a necessary condition for the continuation of cooperative systems.

---

### 3. Contribution and Outcome Coherence

Within cooperative systems, actions generate outcomes that affect the continuation capacity of the system.

For cooperation to remain viable, there must be a meaningful relationship between:

- contribution events  
- observable outcomes  
- and resulting allocation of reward or responsibility  

If this relationship becomes persistently misaligned, participants may reduce or withdraw their contribution. Over time, this weakens the system’s capacity for continuation.

Cooperative systems therefore depend on the continued coherence between contribution and outcome.

---

### 4. Circulation of Participation

The continuation of cooperative systems depends on the ongoing participation of agents.

Participation must circulate through the system in the form of:

- contribution activity  
- role fulfilment  
- and engagement in coordinated processes  

If participation declines or becomes unevenly distributed, parts of the system may become inactive, reducing the overall capacity for coordination.

Conversely, if activity exceeds the system’s capacity to interpret signals or coordinate behaviour, outcomes may become inconsistent and difficult to evaluate.

Cooperative stability therefore depends on the alignment between participation levels and the system’s capacity to interpret and coordinate that participation.

---

### 5. Bounded System Adaptation

Cooperative systems must adapt to changing conditions in order to maintain continuation.

However, changes to system structure, rules, or coordination mechanisms must occur in a manner that preserves existing coordination.

If changes are introduced too rapidly or without consistent interpretation, participants may lose confidence in expectations about future behaviour.

Stable cooperative systems therefore exhibit bounded adaptation, in which changes occur through processes that preserve continuity of coordination.

---

### 6. Continuation Constraint

All cooperative systems operate under the broader constraint of continuation.

A system remains viable only while it retains sufficient capacity to:

- coordinate behaviour  
- absorb variability  
- and respond to environmental pressures  

When these capacities are exceeded, coordination deteriorates and the system may collapse.

Cooperative stability therefore requires that systems operate within conditions that support their continued existence across time.

---

## Interaction of Stability Conditions

These conditions operate jointly rather than independently.

For example:

- signal integrity supports the formation of trust  
- stable coordination depends on reliable signals  
- bounded adaptation preserves signal interpretation  
- participation circulation sustains contribution activity  

Disruption in one condition may propagate through the system and affect overall stability.

---

## Relationship to the Cooperative Boundary

The cooperative boundary defines the threshold at which cooperation becomes possible.

The conditions described in this section apply after that threshold has been crossed.

Together, they describe:

- the transition from coexistence to cooperation (cooperative boundary)  
- the persistence of cooperation across time (cooperative stability)  

---

## Relationship to Temporal Coordination and Trust

Once cooperative stability is established, temporal coordination processes can operate effectively.

Signals generated through contribution events are interpreted by observers, allowing trust judgements to form. These trust judgements accumulate into credibility, which enables the extension of credit and coordinated action across time.

If cooperative stability conditions deteriorate, temporal coordination becomes less reliable, and trust-based coordination may weaken or fail.

---

## Conclusion

The formation of cooperation requires that systems cross the cooperative boundary. The continuation of cooperation requires that systems remain within conditions that preserve coordination across time.

Cooperative stability therefore depends on the coexistence of stable coordination, reliable signals, coherent contribution-outcome relationships, appropriate levels of participation, bounded adaptation, and adherence to continuation constraints.

These conditions define the environment within which cooperative systems are able to persist despite uncertainty and change.
