# Interaction Regimes

Status: Draft

## Introduction

Coexistence systems consist of multiple agents whose actions affect both their own continuation and the continuation of others.

Within such systems, agents do not interact in a single uniform manner. Their actions may enhance, reduce, or have neutral effects on the continuation capacity of other agents.

These differing patterns of interaction form distinct modes through which agents relate to one another. Understanding these modes is necessary for explaining how cooperation emerges and why it does not arise automatically from coexistence alone.

This chapter examines interaction regimes: the structural patterns that characterise how agents affect each other within coexistence systems.

---

## Interaction Regimes

An interaction regime is the dominant pattern by which agents affect each other’s continuation within a coexistence system.

Each regime is defined by the observed effects of agent behaviour rather than by intention or classification. The same agent may operate under different regimes depending on context, time, and the structure of the system.

Interaction regimes describe how agents relate to one another prior to the formation of stable cooperative coordination.

---

## Competition

Competition arises when agents pursue their own continuation under shared constraints.

Within competitive regimes:

- agents operate independently  
- actions are directed toward self-continuation  
- effects on others are indirect and arise through shared resource or capacity constraints  

Competition does not require coordination between agents. Outcomes emerge from the interaction of individual actions within a constrained environment.

Competitive behaviour may persist even when it reduces the continuation capacity of other agents, provided the acting agent’s own continuation is maintained.

---

## Coercion

Coercion arises when one agent constrains or directs the behaviour of another.

Within coercive regimes:

- participation is not fully voluntary  
- one agent imposes conditions on another  
- continuation becomes asymmetrically dependent  

Coercion may sustain coordination in the short term, but it does not rely on mutual alignment of expectations. Instead, it operates through the ability of one agent to enforce behaviour on another.

The persistence of coercion depends on the continued ability to maintain that constraint.

---

## Extraction

Extraction arises when one agent derives benefit at the expense of another’s continuation capacity.

Within extractive regimes:

- value flows from one agent to another without corresponding contribution  
- the affected agent’s capacity for continuation is reduced  
- system-level continuation may degrade over time  

Extraction may persist within a system for extended periods, particularly when its effects are not immediately observable or when agents lack the ability to alter the interaction.

However, sustained extraction reduces the overall capacity of the system to support continued interaction.

---

## Cooperation

Cooperation arises when agents coordinate their actions in ways that support mutual continuation.

Within cooperative regimes:

- agents align behaviour based on expectations of others  
- actions are interdependent  
- outcomes depend on coordinated contribution  

Cooperation does not arise automatically from coexistence. It requires conditions under which agents are willing to rely on the behaviour of others despite uncertainty.

These conditions are defined by the cooperative boundary, which determines when interaction risk is sufficiently reduced to allow coordinated behaviour to emerge.

---

## Transition Between Regimes

Interaction regimes are not fixed.

Systems may transition between regimes as conditions change. For example:

- competitive interaction may evolve into cooperation when coordination becomes beneficial and reliable  
- cooperative systems may revert to competitive or extractive regimes if coordination breaks down  
- coercive structures may give way to cooperation when constraints are replaced by aligned expectations  

The transition from non-cooperative regimes to cooperation depends on the reduction of interaction risk and the formation of expectations about future behaviour.

This transition is examined in the next chapter on the cooperative boundary.

---

## Relationship to Continuation

All interaction regimes operate under the constraint of continuation.

Agents act in ways that affect their own capacity to persist, as well as the capacity of others. Some regimes enhance continuation at the system level, while others may degrade it.

The persistence of a coexistence system does not imply that all interactions are cooperative or beneficial. Multiple regimes may exist simultaneously within the same system.

Understanding how these regimes interact is necessary for explaining how cooperative systems emerge and how they may fail.

---

## Conclusion

Coexistence systems contain multiple interaction regimes through which agents affect each other’s continuation.

Competition, coercion, extraction, and cooperation represent distinct patterns of interaction that may arise under different conditions.

Cooperation is not the default state of interaction. It emerges only when conditions allow agents to coordinate behaviour under uncertainty.

The next chapter examines the cooperative boundary, which defines the threshold at which such coordination becomes possible.
